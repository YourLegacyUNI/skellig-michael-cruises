Since The 6th Century AD

The Most Sacred Rock
in the Atlantic

Irish monks settled on Skellig Michael around 600 AD, carving steps into sheer cliff faces and building beehive huts of dry stone that have stood — without mortar — for over 1,400 years. They came to the edge of the known world to be closer to God.

In 1996, UNESCO declared it a World Heritage Site. It is the most westerly sacred site in Europe, and sits on the ancient St. Michael's Axis — an alignment of sacred sites running from Skellig through France, Italy, Greece, and on to Palestine, known since long before Christianity.

More recently, a galaxy far, far away found its ending here. But the island doesn't care. It has been here before all stories. It will be here after.

Skellig Michael · After Dark · No Light Pollution

Where Monks
Mapped the Stars

On the summit of Skellig Michael, twelve monks once sat in darkness and counted the heavens. At 714 feet above the Atlantic, with no electric light for sixty miles in any direction, the sky above Skellig is among the darkest in Europe. On a clear night, the Milky Way falls like a river over the monastery. The monks would have seen the same stars. This is the rarest kind of silence — one that has lasted fifteen centuries.

Before You Go

What You Need to Know

01

The Crossing

The Atlantic is not decorative. Seas can be rough and weather changes fast. Wear windproof, waterproof layers. Bring food and water, carried in a backpack — your hands need to be free. Budget up to 6 hours for the full experience.

02

The Climb

618 steps. Steep. Uneven. Some with no edge protection. Sturdy walking shoes are not optional — they are required. Take your time. There is no race on Skellig Michael. Children under 12 may not join the Landing Tour.

03

The Island

This is a living wilderness site. Accidents have occurred. An OPW guide meets you at the lighthouse road — follow their instructions without exception. Stay on the path. The walls are 1,400 years old and held together by nothing but skill and time.
